Plenary Address  ·  47th Annual Convention of the Nigerian Institute of Physics  ·  May 2026
"In a World of Robotics and Automation,
Physics is Everything"
Dr Olusola Sayeed Ayoola, PhD.
Chief Executive Officer, Robotics and Artificial Intelligence Nigeria (RAIN)
Lead City University, Ibadan, Oyo State
PROTOCOL

It is a profound honour to stand before you today at this landmark gathering of Nigeria's finest minds.

Opening

When I was in high school, Physics was my favourite subject. I remember the class on linear expansivity — how temperature could cause a measurable change in the length of an object. I remember our lesson on resistivity, and how it too could shift with temperature. These principles, among many others, taught me something early in life that I have never forgotten: this world is governed by laws that will stand the test of time.

And if the world is governed by laws, then searching and researching for more of those laws — more facts, more theories about how our universe operates — will grant us ever greater opportunities to achieve what might otherwise seem unimaginable.

Physics as the foundation of civilisation

From the buoyancy that made sea travel possible, to the discovery of the steam engine, to the harnessing of electricity, and right through to satellite communication — mankind owes an extraordinary debt to the early discoverers of the laws of physics. We owe it to the founding fathers of this noble profession. Every ship that sails, every bulb that lights a room, every voice that travels across a continent through wireless signal — all of it traces back to someone asking a bold question about how the physical world works, and refusing to stop until they found the answer.

Back in high school, we were taught Newton's First Law of Motion: that a body will remain in a state of rest or uniform motion unless acted upon by an external resultant force. That is true of physics. But I have come to believe it is even more profoundly true of nations and of peoples who are seeking change. For a nation to attain technological advancement, its thinkers and its doers must seek fresh ideas, fresh insights, and the courage to apply external force to old ways of doing things. That, distinguished guests, is precisely the job I bring to this conference today.

The title

My address is titled: In a world of robotics and automation, Physics is Everything.

Let me paint you a picture. Imagine a football pitch. On one side, you have the very best human players the world has ever produced — Messi, Ronaldo, the legends of the game. On the other side, you have robots. Now, it does not matter how gifted, how experienced, or how passionate Messi and his teammates are. The robots will calculate the velocity of the ball, the optimal kick force, the angle and trajectory needed to beat every obstacle between them and the post. They will factor in friction, temperature, air resistance, and surface conditions — all in real time, all in milliseconds. And they will score.

But here is the critical point: for us to build robots that perform at that level, we must get our physics right. Because in a world of robotics, where we are dealing with physical objects controlled by the laws of nature, physics is everything.

Why the world is calling for automation

The world today is asking for less human involvement in day-to-day operations — cleaning, cooking, manufacturing, bottling, packaging, delivery of goods, driving, and increasingly, even healthcare. The generation of today wants everything fast, easy, and reliable. People are becoming more introverted. They want to relate less with individuals who may judge them, and more with systems that are consistent, tireless, and impartial. They want to be served by artificial intelligence — by cashiers, doctors, and government systems devoid of human sentiment and personality bias.

What this tells us, indirectly, is that humanity is calling for the world to operate itself. Society is reaching back to physics in search of self-aware physical objects guided by natural laws and acting in our interest, regardless of our own inadequacies. This is the very essence of robotics and automation.

And the need has never been more urgent. The COVID-19 pandemic of 2019–2020 was a testament to how fragile our human-dependent systems truly are. From sanitiser-spraying drones to robot-assisted clinics, from intelligent self-operating ventilators to self-driving vehicles enforcing social distancing across our cities — robotics evolved during that crisis from a manufacturing tool into a societal necessity. The world woke up. We cannot afford to go back to sleep.

Where physics lives inside robotics

Let me now walk you through the domains where physics is not merely relevant — it is indispensable.

Autonomous vehicles. The most significant challenge facing self-driving cars is detection and response. Can the vehicle determine, quickly and accurately, that an obstacle lies ahead? Can it estimate speed and calculate precisely how to decelerate in order to stop safely at the right distance? This is classical physics — speed, acceleration, time, and force. But detecting the obstacle in the first place — whether through optical sensors, LiDAR, or infrared — that too is physics. And yet the problem does not end there. When weather changes, visibility drops, optical sensors degrade, and infrared readings become unreliable. Road surfaces become slippery; friction coefficients vary. For self-driving vehicles to truly work at scale, we need advanced physics. We need smarter sensor science, improved understanding of material interactions with light and energy waves, and the ability to fuse data from multiple sources in real time. The discovery of graphene, for instance, has already revolutionised how we think about materials in electronics and sensor design. This is what smart physics looks like in the real world.

Humanoid robots. When we design a robot that walks, reaches, lifts, and balances like a human being, the kinematics of every joint must be precisely calculated. The distribution of torque, the centre of gravity across varying postures, the dynamic stabilisation of a bipedal structure in motion — all of this draws on classical mechanics, rigid body dynamics, and control theory. A humanoid robot is not just a machine. It is a walking, breathing physics problem.

Underwater robots. Here, acoustic positioning using sonar and sound wave propagation replaces optical sensing. Wireless signal transmission through water presents unique challenges because electromagnetic waves behave very differently underwater than in air. Power delivery to untethered subaquatic systems is a frontier problem in physics. Getting an underwater robot to navigate, communicate, and operate reliably in deep-sea conditions demands mastery of wave mechanics, pressure physics, and materials science.

Robot arms and industrial systems. In manufacturing, the precision of a robotic arm depends on torque calculation, moment of inertia, and materials science. The choice of end-effector material, the tolerances in motion, the response to load — all governed by physics. The reliability of an industrial robot is ultimately a statement about how well its designers understood the physical world.

Drones. Whether fixed-wing or multi-rotor, drone flight is applied aerodynamics. Lift, drag, thrust, and weight are in constant negotiation with every gust of wind and every change in altitude. Navigation requires an understanding of GPS signal physics, inertial measurement, and increasingly, computer vision. For a delivery drone to arrive at your doorstep safely, physics must have been solved many times over.

Smart homes and the Internet of Things. The physics of smart metering, remote sensing, wireless connectivity, battery energy storage, and power management underpins every smart home system. When the power goes out, a truly smart system must still be active — which means energy storage, charge-discharge physics, and thermal management are all at play. IoT is not just about software. Every sensor node, every signal, every edge device is a physics instrument.

Technology advancing physics in return

Now, it would be incomplete to speak only of how physics enables technology. The relationship is reciprocal, and I think it is important that we acknowledge this.

Technology has given physics gifts that would have seemed miraculous to our predecessors. The ability to process vast amounts of experimental data using artificial intelligence and machine learning has accelerated discovery in ways we are only beginning to understand. High-performance computing has made it possible to simulate physical systems — from molecular interactions to cosmic dynamics — at levels of fidelity that no laboratory alone could achieve. Space exploration has given us data from planetary bodies and cosmic environments that are rewriting our understanding of gravity, radiation, and the nature of matter itself.

Virtual reality is enabling new modes of laboratory simulation. Wearable technology and IoT sensors are generating real-world, real-time data streams that dwarf what any controlled experiment could produce. Quantum computing is beginning to unlock new computational paradigms that will further accelerate physical modelling. And collaborative platforms for cross-border data sharing are enabling the kind of global scientific conversation that can take us, together, much further than any of us could go alone.

All of this tells us one thing clearly: the era of the isolated laboratory experiment, conducted under tightly controlled conditions and then published months later, is giving way to something more dynamic, more continuous, and more powerful. We are entering the age of smart physics.

The call to action

And so I come now to my charge — not just to this distinguished audience of physicists, but to the Nigerian Institute of Physics as an institution, and to Nigeria as a nation with both the need and the potential to lead.

Smart physics is not a slogan. It is a methodology. It means making our laboratories smarter — equipping them with the sensors, computing tools, and data management systems that allow us to gather richer, more accurate data. It means moving our research beyond controlled indoor environments and into the field, into real-world conditions, into living systems that cannot be paused for our convenience. It means conducting experiments while the system is running, not shutting down operations just to take a reading.

It means collaboration — genuine, open, cross-institutional collaboration. Sharing data, sharing resources, and building repositories of Nigerian and African physical science knowledge that can stand alongside the best in the world.

And crucially, it means applying physics in service of our most pressing national challenges. How can smart physics improve agricultural yield — from precision irrigation to soil sensing? How can it strengthen border and community security — from acoustic intrusion detection to drone surveillance systems? How can it support industrialisation and improve our management of energy resources? These are not theoretical questions. They are urgent national imperatives, and physics is the language in which their solutions will be written.

Conclusion

Distinguished ladies and gentlemen, technological advancement is not an end in itself. It is a means — a means to feed more people, to protect more lives, to build a stronger, more self-reliant nation. Physics is the foundation beneath all of it.

We began this journey when someone noticed that heat could change the length of a metal rod. We continued it when someone asked why an apple falls downward and not upward. We will take the next leap when Nigerian physicists — in this room and beyond — apply the full power of their expertise to the robotics and automation challenges of our time.

In a world of robotics and automation, physics is not background knowledge. Physics is not an academic exercise. Physics is everything.

Thank you.

Dr Olusola Sayeed Ayoola, PhD.  ·  CEO, Robotics and Artificial Intelligence Nigeria (RAIN)  ·  NIP 47th Annual Convention, May 2026
