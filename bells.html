▸ PILLAR I — FOUNDATIONS OF MODERN COMPUTING (Weeks 1–3)
WK 01
Course Intro & Why Old Computing Stopped Working
⏱ ~7 min to teach
⌄
FoundationsHistory
📌 What This Chapter Is About

For 50 years, computers got faster automatically — engineers just made transistors smaller and everything sped up. Around 2004, that stopped working. This chapter explains why it stopped and what engineers are doing about it. This is the "why" behind everything else in the course.

📖 Your Teaching Summary (~7 min)

Imagine a highway. For decades, we kept adding lanes (more transistors) and cars automatically went faster. Then we hit a problem: the highway started catching fire because we added too many cars. That is literally what happened to computer chips — they got so hot they could not run safely.

This is called the Power Wall. To deal with it, chip designers had to turn off parts of the chip to stop overheating. A chip where large sections are intentionally switched off is called a chip with Dark Silicon — silicon that exists but cannot be used.

There is a second problem: even when the processor is fast, it sits around waiting for data from memory, because memory is much slower. This gap between processor speed and memory speed is called the Memory Wall. The whole course is about creative solutions to these two walls.

🔑 Key Terms — Explained Simply
Dennard Scaling
💡 Analogy: Making a smaller car engine that uses the same fuel as before
A rule from 1974 that said: if you shrink a transistor, it uses proportionally less power, so you can add more transistors without using more electricity. This worked beautifully for 30 years. Around 2004, it broke — smaller transistors started leaking electricity like a cracked pipe, generating heat instead of doing useful work.
Power Wall
💡 Analogy: A room with so many light bulbs it catches fire
When chips got too dense, they generated more heat than cooling systems could remove. You cannot run a chip at full speed when it is overheating — so chips hit a speed ceiling. This is the Power Wall: chips cannot simply run faster because they would melt.
Dark Silicon
💡 Analogy: A skyscraper where you can only turn on lights in a few floors at a time or the electrical system fails
Because of the Power Wall, modern chips cannot power all their transistors simultaneously. Large portions of the chip are kept switched off (dark) to manage heat. We are building chips with billions of transistors but only using a fraction at any moment. This is wasteful but currently unavoidable.
Memory Wall
💡 Analogy: A genius professor who can think 100 times faster than anyone can hand him paper
Modern processors can do billions of operations per second, but RAM (memory) can only supply data a fraction of that speed. The processor spends most of its time doing nothing — just waiting for data. This waiting is the Memory Wall, and it is arguably a bigger problem than processor speed today.
Amdahl's Law
💡 Analogy: You speed up 90% of a road trip by flying, but you still have to drive the last 10% — total time savings are limited
If 10% of a program cannot be parallelised (run simultaneously), then no matter how many processors you add, you can never make the program more than 10 times faster. It is a mathematical ceiling on parallel speedup. Named after Gene Amdahl who proved this in 1967.
🎓 How to Teach This in 7 Minutes
  • Open with: "For 50 years computers got faster for free — today they don't. Why?" (1 min)
  • Explain Dennard Scaling broke → Power Wall → Dark Silicon using the highway/fire analogy (3 min)
  • Explain the Memory Wall using the fast professor waiting for paper (2 min)
  • Close with: "Everything else in this course is how engineers are solving these two problems" (1 min)

WK 02
New Ways to Build Processors (Architecture I)
⏱ ~7 min to teach
⌄
Architecture
📌 What This Chapter Is About

Since we cannot make individual processors faster, engineers got creative about how processors are built and connected. This chapter covers three major innovations: building chips from small pieces (chiplets), stacking memory on top of the processor, and a free open-source processor design anyone can use.

📖 Your Teaching Summary (~7 min)

Think of old chips like baking one giant cake. If one part burns, you throw the whole cake away — very wasteful. The new approach — Chiplets — is like assembling a cake from smaller pieces baked separately. Each piece is cheaper to make, and if one fails, you replace just that piece. AMD uses this for their Ryzen processors and it is why they became competitive with Intel.

The second innovation is HBM (High Bandwidth Memory) — instead of putting memory far away on a circuit board, engineers physically stack memory chips directly on top of the processor like floors of a building. Data travels a tiny distance instead of a long journey across the board. This solves a big chunk of the Memory Wall problem.

The third big development is RISC-V — an open, free instruction set (the language a processor speaks) that anyone can use without paying licensing fees to companies like Intel or ARM. Universities, startups, and entire countries (China, India) are building processors using RISC-V.

🔑 Key Terms — Explained Simply
Chiplets
💡 Analogy: Building a LEGO city from separate pre-made modules instead of carving it from one block of stone
Instead of manufacturing one giant chip that is hard and expensive to make correctly, engineers build several smaller chips (chiplets) and connect them in one package. Each chiplet does a specific job — one handles calculations, one handles memory, one handles I/O. This approach is cheaper, more flexible, and easier to manufacture at high quality.
HBM — High Bandwidth Memory
💡 Analogy: Instead of your filing cabinet being across the room, it is built into your desk — you can grab files 100x faster
Traditional memory (RAM) is located on separate chips connected by long wires on a circuit board. HBM stacks memory dies (layers) vertically, right beside or on top of the processor chip. Because data travels micrometres instead of centimetres, you get enormously more data throughput — critical for AI and graphics workloads that are starved for data.
RISC-V (pronounced "risk five")
💡 Analogy: Like the Linux of processor design — free, open-source, anyone can build their own version
Every processor speaks a language called an Instruction Set Architecture (ISA). Intel has x86, ARM has their ISA — and you pay to use them. RISC-V is a free, open ISA. Universities can teach using it, startups can build chips on it, and governments can design national processors without depending on foreign companies. It is simple, clean, and increasingly powerful.
Processing-in-Memory (PIM)
💡 Analogy: Instead of bringing ingredients from the farm to the kitchen, you cook at the farm
Rather than moving data from memory to the processor for calculation, PIM puts simple calculation circuits inside the memory itself. This eliminates the data journey entirely. It is especially useful for AI and database workloads where data movement is the dominant cost in time and energy.
🎓 How to Teach This in 7 Minutes
  • Chiplets: "We stopped making one big chip — we assemble from parts." LEGO analogy. AMD Ryzen example. (3 min)
  • HBM: "We moved memory to sit right on top of the processor." Filing cabinet analogy. (2 min)
  • RISC-V: "There is now a free, open-source processor language anyone can use." Linux comparison. (2 min)

WK 03
How Processors Actually Think (Architecture II)
⏱ ~7 min to teach
⌄
Architecture
📌 What This Chapter Is About

This chapter goes inside the processor to understand how it executes instructions so efficiently — and why those same techniques accidentally created massive security holes (which you cover in Week 8). It also introduces the idea of building a custom chip for one specific job instead of a general one.

📖 Your Teaching Summary (~7 min)

Modern processors are much smarter than they look. When they encounter an "if/else" decision in your code, instead of waiting to find out which direction the program goes, they guess and start running ahead. This is called Speculative Execution. If the guess is right — great, no time wasted. If wrong, they undo the work and take the correct path. This makes processors dramatically faster.

The Roofline Model is a tool engineers use to diagnose whether a program is being slowed down by the processor being too slow (compute-bound) or by memory being too slow (memory-bound). Think of it as a doctor's diagnostic test — before fixing a performance problem, you identify which type it is.

Finally, this week introduces Domain-Specific Architectures — chips built for exactly one type of job. Google's chip for AI (the TPU) can do AI calculations 100x more efficiently than a general processor because every part of it is designed just for that task, nothing wasted on flexibility.

🔑 Key Terms — Explained Simply
Speculative Execution
💡 Analogy: A chef who starts preparing both steak AND chicken while a customer is still deciding, then throws away the one not ordered
When a processor hits an "if-then" branch in code, it predicts which path will be taken and starts executing instructions along that path before knowing the answer. If right, no time is lost. If wrong, it discards the speculative work and retries. Modern CPUs guess correctly about 95% of the time, making programs run dramatically faster. However — this creates a famous security problem covered in Week 8.
Out-of-Order Execution
💡 Analogy: A restaurant kitchen that starts cooking your dessert while your main course is still finishing, instead of waiting
Processors do not wait for one instruction to fully complete before starting the next if the next instruction does not depend on the first. They identify independent instructions and run them simultaneously or in a different order. The result appears correct to the program, but throughput is dramatically higher.
Roofline Model
💡 Analogy: A doctor measuring both your heart rate AND blood pressure — two different ceilings, one is always the limit
A simple visual tool that plots a program's performance against two ceilings: the maximum computation speed (compute roof) and the maximum data delivery speed from memory (memory bandwidth roof). Your program will always be limited by whichever ceiling it hits first. This tells engineers whether to buy a faster processor or more memory bandwidth to improve performance.
Domain-Specific Architecture (DSA)
💡 Analogy: A specialist surgeon vs. a general practitioner — the specialist is 100x better at one thing because they do nothing else
A chip designed to do exactly one category of tasks extremely well, sacrificing flexibility. Google's TPU only does the matrix mathematics needed for AI — but it does it 100x more efficiently per watt than a general CPU. As general-purpose scaling ends, building specialised chips is how engineers keep improving performance. This is called the "New Golden Age" of computer architecture.
🎓 How to Teach This in 7 Minutes
  • Speculative execution: Chef preparing two meals analogy. "This is how CPUs stay fast — and also how they got hacked." (3 min)
  • Roofline Model: Doctor diagnostic analogy — "is the problem compute or memory?" (2 min)
  • DSA: Specialist surgeon analogy. "Google built a chip only for AI — 100x better." (2 min)

WK 04
Using Many Different Chips Together (Heterogeneous Computing)
⏱ ~7 min to teach
⌄
ArchitectureAI/ML
📌 What This Chapter Is About

Since one type of processor cannot do everything efficiently, modern systems combine different types — a CPU for general tasks, a GPU for parallel tasks, an FPGA for custom logic. This chapter explains what each type is, what it is best at, and how they work together.

📖 Your Teaching Summary (~7 min)

Think of a construction site. You would not use a hammer to pour concrete or a cement mixer to drive nails. Different tools for different jobs — that is heterogeneous computing. A CPU (Central Processing Unit) is the general-purpose processor — very fast at sequential tasks, smart, but limited in how many things it can do at once. A GPU (Graphics Processing Unit) was originally built for video games but turns out to be perfect for AI — it has thousands of simple cores that all do the same operation on different data simultaneously. A FPGA is a chip you can reprogram — like a blank circuit board that becomes whatever circuit you want. This makes it perfect when the task is known but you need flexibility to update it later.

The key insight: no single chip type wins at everything. Modern AI systems use all three simultaneously — a CPU orchestrates everything, a GPU runs the AI model, and FPGAs might handle network processing.

🔑 Key Terms — Explained Simply
CPU (Central Processing Unit)
💡 Analogy: A brilliant professor who can only teach one student at a time, but teaches anything
The main processor in any computer. It has a small number of very powerful cores (usually 4–64) that execute instructions one after another at extremely high speed. Excellent for complex logic, running operating systems, and any task that has many sequential steps that depend on each other. Not good at doing millions of identical simple tasks simultaneously.
GPU (Graphics Processing Unit)
💡 Analogy: 10,000 students all doing simple arithmetic simultaneously — perfect for AI and graphics
Originally designed to draw video game pixels (each pixel needs the same colour calculation), GPUs have thousands of simple cores that can all do the same operation on different data simultaneously. This is called SIMT — Single Instruction, Multiple Threads. It turns out AI training is exactly this pattern: apply the same mathematical operation to millions of numbers. This is why NVIDIA became the most valuable chip company in the world.
FPGA (Field-Programmable Gate Array)
💡 Analogy: A blank circuit board that turns into whatever circuit you program it to be — like programmable hardware
An FPGA is a chip made of millions of tiny logic blocks that can be connected in any pattern by software. You literally define what circuit exists inside it. This means you can customise it for any task with hardware-level speed, but unlike an ASIC (fixed chip), you can reprogram it if requirements change. Microsoft uses FPGAs in data centres to accelerate specific tasks like search ranking and network processing.
SIMT — Single Instruction, Multiple Threads
💡 Analogy: An army marching in lockstep — everyone takes the same step at the same time, but each soldier is in a different position
The GPU execution model. All threads in a group (called a "warp" of 32 threads) execute the exact same instruction simultaneously, but each one operates on different data. This is how a GPU can do 10,000 AI calculations at once. The weakness: if threads need to do different things (branch divergence), half the GPU sits idle waiting for the other half.
Systolic Array
💡 Analogy: An assembly line where every worker passes their result to the next worker in rhythm — like a mathematical conveyor belt
A grid of simple multiplication-accumulation units that pass data between each other in a fixed rhythm (like a heartbeat — "systolic" from "systole"). Data flows through the grid and gets multiplied and added as it goes, performing matrix multiplication efficiently. Google's TPU chip is built entirely around a 256×256 systolic array. Matrix multiplication is the core operation of all AI — that is why TPUs are so fast for AI.
🎓 How to Teach This in 7 Minutes
  • Start: "Different jobs need different tools — construction site analogy." (1 min)
  • CPU: Brilliant professor who can only help one student. (1 min)
  • GPU: 10,000 students doing simple arithmetic simultaneously. Explain why AI loves this. (3 min)
  • FPGA: Blank circuit board you program into anything. Microsoft data centre example. (2 min)

WK 05
GPU Chips & AI Hardware — How ChatGPT is Actually Run
⏱ ~7 min to teach
⌄
ArchitectureAI/ML
📌 What This Chapter Is About

This chapter focuses on the specific hardware that runs AI — from NVIDIA's GPU Tensor Cores to Google's TPU chips. It explains why AI is so hardware-hungry, and what innovations make large language models like ChatGPT possible to run at scale.

📖 Your Teaching Summary (~7 min)

Running ChatGPT or any large AI model requires enormous amounts of hardware. Here is why: AI models are made of billions of numbers (called weights or parameters). To generate one response, the system must load all those numbers from memory and multiply them together. The bottleneck is not computation speed — it is how fast you can move data from memory to the processor. This is why AI chips need special memory technology like HBM (from Chapter 2).

NVIDIA's Tensor Cores are special circuits inside their GPUs designed specifically for the matrix multiplications AI needs — they can do this operation in one clock cycle instead of many. Google's TPU goes further: the entire chip is a matrix multiplier with almost no flexibility for other tasks — but it is absurdly efficient for AI. When you use ChatGPT, your question is processed across a cluster of thousands of these chips connected by NVLink, NVIDIA's ultra-fast chip-to-chip connection, allowing thousands of GPUs to work as one giant computer.

🔑 Key Terms — Explained Simply
Tensor Core
💡 Analogy: A specialised calculator that does one specific complex calculation instantly instead of step-by-step
A special hardware unit inside NVIDIA GPUs (starting from the Volta generation in 2017) that performs "D = A×B + C" — a matrix multiply-accumulate — in a single clock cycle using reduced precision numbers (FP16 or BF16). Standard GPU cores would take many cycles for the same operation. Tensor Cores are why modern AI training is possible — they deliver 100s of TFLOPS on AI workloads.
TPU — Tensor Processing Unit
💡 Analogy: A factory production line that only makes one product — extremely fast and efficient, but cannot make anything else
Google's custom AI chip. Unlike a GPU which can do many things, a TPU is built purely for matrix multiplication (the core AI operation). It uses a large systolic array (explained in Ch.4) and dedicated high-bandwidth memory. TPUs power all of Google's AI services. They are so efficient that Google's TPU v4 can train large AI models 10x faster per dollar than GPU clusters.
NVLink
💡 Analogy: A private motorway between buildings versus a slow public road — data moves 10x faster between GPUs
NVIDIA's proprietary high-speed connection between GPU chips. Standard connections (PCIe) transfer data at around 64 GB/s between chips. NVLink in the H100 does 900 GB/s — over 14x faster. This matters because AI training requires constant sharing of gradient information between GPUs. With NVLink, thousands of GPUs can act as a single, coherent, enormous accelerator.
Memory Bandwidth (in AI context)
💡 Analogy: A library where reading books is instant, but physically walking to the shelf takes most of your time
AI inference (running a model to get answers) is mostly memory-bandwidth-bound — the chip waits for model weights to arrive from memory. A 70-billion parameter AI model needs ~140 GB just to store the numbers. Getting all those numbers to the compute units fast enough is the primary engineering challenge. This is why AI chips use HBM memory and why memory capacity and bandwidth are listed on AI chip spec sheets, not just FLOPS.
🎓 How to Teach This in 7 Minutes
  • "ChatGPT lives on thousands of chips — let me show you what those chips look like." (1 min)
  • Tensor Cores: Specialised calculator — one clock cycle for what used to take many. (2 min)
  • TPU: Factory that makes one product only — absurdly efficient. (2 min)
  • NVLink: Private motorway between GPUs. Why 1000 GPUs act as one giant computer. (2 min)

WK 06
Computing at the Edge — Bringing AI to Your Phone & Sensors
⏱ ~7 min to teach
⌄
Edge/IoTAI/ML
📌 What This Chapter Is About

Not all computing can happen in a distant data centre. Sometimes data needs to be processed right where it is created — on a phone, a camera, a hospital sensor. This is called edge computing. This chapter explains why it matters, how AI fits into tiny devices, and a clever privacy-preserving way to train AI without sending your data anywhere.

📖 Your Teaching Summary (~7 min)

Imagine a self-driving car that needs to identify a pedestrian and brake immediately. Sending that camera image to a server in Lagos, waiting for a response, and then braking — by then the accident has already happened. The computation must happen locally, instantly. That is Edge Computing: processing data at or near its source, not in a distant cloud.

TinyML is the art of squeezing AI into impossibly small devices — microcontrollers with 256KB of RAM and no internet connection. Engineers compress models by converting precise numbers to simpler ones (quantisation) and cutting out unused parts (pruning) until the model fits. Your phone already does this: Siri, Google Assistant, and face unlock all run on-device AI chips.

Federated Learning solves a critical privacy problem: how do you train an AI on sensitive medical data from a thousand hospitals without sending that data to one central server? Answer: the model goes to each hospital, trains locally on their private data, then only the learned adjustments (not the data) are sent back and averaged. Private data never leaves.

🔑 Key Terms — Explained Simply
Edge Computing
💡 Analogy: Processing fish at the harbour instead of shipping raw fish to a factory inland and back
Computing that happens close to where data is generated — on a device, a local gateway, or a nearby mini data centre — rather than sending all data to a faraway cloud. Reduces latency (waiting time), saves bandwidth costs, and allows operation even without internet. Essential for autonomous vehicles, industrial robots, remote sensing, and any real-time system.
TinyML
💡 Analogy: Fitting a full orchestra performance into a tiny music box — you have to cleverly compress everything down
Machine Learning deployed on microcontrollers — chips so small and cheap they are in light switches and thermostats. A TinyML model might use only 20KB of storage and run on a coin battery for a year. It achieves this through extreme compression: quantisation (turning 32-bit numbers into 8-bit numbers), pruning (removing unnecessary connections), and knowledge distillation (training a small model to mimic a big one).
Quantisation
💡 Analogy: Rounding prices to the nearest dollar instead of exact cents — a bit less precise, but the shopping still works fine
AI model weights are normally stored as 32-bit floating-point numbers (very precise). Quantisation converts them to 8-bit or even 4-bit integers (much less precise but 4–8x smaller). The model becomes dramatically smaller and faster, with only a tiny loss in accuracy. This is how a model too large for your phone becomes small enough to run on it.
Federated Learning
💡 Analogy: Every hospital improves a shared medical textbook locally from their own patients, but never shares the patient records themselves — only their editorial suggestions
A way to train AI models across many devices or institutions without sharing the underlying private data. Each participant trains the model locally, computes the changes the training would make (gradients), and sends only those small changes to a central coordinator. The coordinator averages all changes and sends back the improved model. Private data never leaves each participant. Google uses this for keyboard autocomplete — your typing improves the model without Google seeing your messages.
🎓 How to Teach This in 7 Minutes
  • Self-driving car scenario: "It cannot wait for the cloud." Introduce Edge Computing. (2 min)
  • TinyML: Music box analogy. "We compress AI until it fits on a chip smaller than your thumbnail." (2 min)
  • Federated Learning: Hospital analogy. "Private data never leaves, only the lessons learned." (3 min)

WK 07
Internet of Things — Connecting Everything to Everything
⏱ ~7 min to teach
⌄
Edge/IoTSystems
📌 What This Chapter Is About

The Internet of Things (IoT) is the network of physical objects — lights, locks, fridges, farm sensors, factory machines — that talk to each other and to the internet. This chapter covers the special communication protocols designed for tiny, battery-powered devices, and the emerging standard that is trying to unify all smart devices.

📖 Your Teaching Summary (~7 min)

Billions of devices now connect to the internet — not computers or phones, but sensors, switches, meters, and machines. These devices are often tiny, battery-powered, and need to send small amounts of data occasionally. Normal internet protocols (like HTTP, what your browser uses) are too heavy for them — like using a lorry to deliver a single letter.

MQTT was designed for exactly this: an extremely lightweight messaging protocol where devices publish messages to a central broker (a middleman server), and other devices subscribe to topics they care about. A temperature sensor publishes its reading; an air conditioner subscribes and reacts. Simple, fast, low energy.

Matter is the new industry standard (backed by Apple, Google, Amazon, Samsung) to make all smart home devices speak the same language. Before Matter, a Samsung smart bulb could not talk to an Apple HomeKit system. Matter solves this: one protocol for all smart home devices.

Security is the IoT crisis: most IoT devices have default passwords never changed, no update mechanism, and run software years out of date. When millions of these devices were hijacked in 2016 (the Mirai botnet), they took down Twitter, Netflix, and PayPal across the USA.

🔑 Key Terms — Explained Simply
IoT — Internet of Things
💡 Analogy: Giving every physical object in the world a voice so it can report its status and receive instructions
The network of physical devices embedded with sensors, software, and connectivity that allows them to collect and exchange data. Includes smart home devices, industrial sensors (factory temperature, vibration, pressure), medical monitoring devices, agricultural sensors, smart city infrastructure, and wearables. Estimated 15+ billion connected devices globally today, projected to reach 75 billion by 2030.
MQTT — Message Queuing Telemetry Transport
💡 Analogy: A notice board where devices post updates, and others read only the updates they care about — nobody talks directly to each other
A lightweight publish-subscribe messaging protocol designed in 1999 for oil pipeline monitoring where sensors had limited bandwidth. A central server called a "broker" receives messages from publishers (sensors) and forwards them to subscribers (controllers or apps) who have expressed interest. Uses minimal data per message — as low as 2 bytes header. Runs on top of TCP/IP. Standard protocol for most IoT systems and industrial automation.
CoAP — Constrained Application Protocol
💡 Analogy: A stripped-down text message version of the full internet web request system — for devices too small for the full thing
Where MQTT uses a broker for publish/subscribe, CoAP works more like web browsing (request/response) but designed for devices with only kilobytes of RAM. Uses UDP (not TCP) — which uses less overhead. Created specifically for "constrained" devices like microcontrollers. Often used in smart energy meters, building automation, and healthcare sensors where you need request/response behaviour without HTTP's overhead.
LoRaWAN
💡 Analogy: A very quiet radio whisper that travels kilometres — you cannot send much information but you can send it very far on a tiny battery
Long Range Wide Area Network. A wireless protocol that can transmit small amounts of data over distances of 2–15km using very low power — a single AA battery can last 5–10 years. Used for smart city sensors (parking, air quality), agricultural monitoring (soil moisture across vast farms), and utility metering in areas without cellular coverage. The tradeoff: extremely low data rate (only a few hundred bytes per second).
Matter Protocol
💡 Analogy: A universal power plug adapter — one standard that works everywhere, regardless of who made the device
An open-source smart home connectivity standard backed by the Connectivity Standards Alliance (Apple, Google, Amazon, Samsung, and 200+ others). Runs on IPv6 over Wi-Fi or Thread (a low-power mesh network protocol). A Matter-certified device can be set up and controlled by any Matter-compatible hub — Apple Home, Google Home, Amazon Alexa — regardless of brand. Released in 2022, now the de-facto smart home interoperability standard.
🎓 How to Teach This in 7 Minutes
  • "Normal internet protocols are too heavy for tiny sensors — like using a lorry to deliver one letter." (1 min)
  • MQTT: Notice board analogy. Sensors post, controllers subscribe. (2 min)
  • LoRaWAN: "A whisper that travels 10km on a battery lasting 5 years — perfect for farm sensors." (2 min)
  • Matter: Universal plug adapter. "Finally all smart home devices speak one language." (1 min)
  • Security crisis: Mirai botnet — 2016 — took down Twitter from hacked cameras and routers. (1 min)

▸ PILLAR III — HARDWARE SECURITY & REAL-TIME SYSTEMS (Weeks 8–10)
WK 08
When Hardware Gets Hacked — Spectre, Meltdown & Trusted Chips
⏱ ~7 min to teach
⌄
SecurityArchitecture
📌 What This Chapter Is About

In 2018, security researchers discovered that the same performance tricks that make CPUs fast (from Chapter 3 — speculative execution) also create catastrophic security holes. This chapter explains those attacks in plain English and covers the hardware solutions engineers built to restore trust.

📖 Your Teaching Summary (~7 min)

Remember from Chapter 3: processors guess which way code will go and run ahead speculatively. Here is the problem. Even when the CPU realises it guessed wrong and "undoes" the speculative work — the data it touched has already been loaded into the cache (a small, fast memory). The cache state is a "footprint" that an attacker can detect by carefully timing how long memory reads take. Spectre exploits this to read data across security boundaries — one program can spy on data belonging to another program. Meltdown used the same principle to completely break the wall between the operating system kernel and normal programs, letting a regular program read kernel memory directly.

These attacks affected virtually every Intel, AMD, and ARM processor made in the past 20 years. The fixes — KPTI (kernel page table isolation) and Retpoline — work by essentially restricting the speculative execution that made CPUs so fast, costing up to 30% performance penalty.

The response: build Trusted Execution Environments (TEEs) — isolated, hardware-encrypted zones inside the CPU where even the operating system cannot read your code or data. Intel's version is called SGX (Software Guard Extensions). Think of it as a private vault inside the CPU that even the bank manager (the OS) cannot open.

🔑 Key Terms — Explained Simply
Spectre Attack
💡 Analogy: You train a bank teller to automatically start pulling out money when they see someone in a mask — then you use that reaction to infer what is in the safe, even without opening it
Spectre tricks the CPU's branch predictor (its guessing mechanism) into speculatively reading secret data from memory. Even though the CPU cancels this read, the secret data was briefly loaded into the cache. The attacker then probes the cache timing to figure out what was read. Named "Spectre" because it is caused by speculative execution — a ghost of computation that should not have happened. Affects virtually all modern processors. Has many variants (Spectre v1, v2, v4, etc.), each slightly different.
Meltdown Attack
💡 Analogy: A library rule says students cannot access the restricted section, but due to a loophole, they can briefly glimpse the books before being thrown out — and that glimpse is enough
Meltdown exploits the fact that processors perform memory accesses (including to restricted kernel memory) before checking whether they have permission. The permission check triggers an error and the access is cancelled — but the data was briefly in the cache. An attacker can read that cached data via timing. Meltdown completely broke user/kernel memory isolation. It was fully patched with KPTI, which adds an overhead of 5–30% performance loss on Intel chips.
Cache Side-Channel Attack
💡 Analogy: You cannot see what someone is reading in the library, but by measuring how long they take to find a book, you can infer which shelf they went to
A technique that extracts secret information by measuring the time difference between cache hits (fast — data was already cached) and cache misses (slow — data had to be fetched from RAM). This timing difference is a "side channel" — information leaked through the physical implementation rather than through the program's intended behaviour. Flush+Reload is the most common variant: flush a cache line, wait for the victim to run, then time how fast you can reload it — fast means the victim loaded it for you.
Intel SGX — Software Guard Extensions
💡 Analogy: A sealed vault inside the bank that even the bank manager cannot open — only the person who owns the vault knows what is inside
A CPU feature that creates encrypted, isolated regions of memory called "enclaves." Code and data inside an enclave are encrypted by hardware — even the operating system, hypervisor, or BIOS cannot read them. The processor decrypts data only inside its own processing units, never exposing plaintext outside the enclave boundary. Used for confidential cloud computing, digital rights management, secure key storage, and private AI inference. Has been compromised by several attacks (Foreshadow, SGAxe) demonstrating that hardware security requires constant vigilance.
TPM — Trusted Platform Module
💡 Analogy: A tamper-proof ID card that proves your computer is genuine and has not been tampered with — like a passport for your hardware
A dedicated security chip (or firmware implementation) on the motherboard that provides hardware-level cryptographic functions: secure key storage (private keys never leave the TPM), measured boot (records a hash of every software component at startup to detect tampering), and remote attestation (cryptographically proves to a remote server that the system is in a known-good state). Windows 11 requires TPM 2.0. Used in enterprise security, BitLocker encryption, and confidential computing.

  • Spectre: Bank teller analogy — trained reflex reveals secret without ever opening the safe. (2 min)
  • Meltdown: Library loophole analogy — brief glimpse before being thrown out is enough. (1 min)
  • SGX: Bank vault analogy — even the OS cannot see inside. "Used for private AI in the cloud." (2 min)
  • TPM: Passport for hardware — proves your computer is genuine. "Windows 11 requires this." (1 min)

WK 09
Spying Through Side Channels — Power, Signals & Memory Attacks
⏱ ~7 min to teach
⌄
Security
📌 What This Chapter Is About

Beyond cache timing attacks, hardware leaks information in other physical ways: through power consumption, electromagnetic radiation, and even by physically corrupting memory cells. This chapter covers these physical side-channel attacks and the hardware attack that can flip bits in RAM without ever "accessing" the target memory.

📖 Your Teaching Summary (~7 min)

When a computer performs a cryptographic operation, it uses more or less electricity depending on what data it is processing. This seems irrelevant — but attackers can record these tiny power variations using an oscilloscope and mathematically extract the secret encryption key. This is Power Analysis. Versions of it have been used to steal keys from bank cards, smart meters, and military devices.

Similarly, every electronic circuit radiates a tiny electromagnetic field that varies with its computation. With a sensitive antenna placed near a device, an attacker can capture this radiation and extract data — without touching the device at all. This is an Electromagnetic (EM) Side-Channel Attack.

Rowhammer is in a category of its own. Modern RAM is so densely packed that repeatedly reading from one row causes electrical interference that flips bits in adjacent rows — corrupting data in memory you never accessed. Attackers have used this to flip a single bit in a page table (the map of who owns which memory) and escalate from a normal user to having full administrator access.

🔑 Key Terms — Explained Simply
Power Analysis Attack
💡 Analogy: Watching an electrician's power meter fluctuate as they work to figure out exactly what they are doing inside a sealed room
Measuring the power consumed by a device while it performs cryptographic operations to extract secret keys. In Simple Power Analysis (SPA), a single trace can reveal the key directly. In Differential Power Analysis (DPA), thousands of traces are statistically analysed to filter out noise. DPA can crack AES encryption on a smart card with off-the-shelf equipment costing a few hundred dollars. Countermeasures include "masking" (adding random noise to operations) and "balancing" (making all operations consume the same power).
Electromagnetic (EM) Side-Channel Attack
💡 Analogy: Holding a sensitive microphone outside a closed room and listening to what kind of work is happening inside from the sounds of machines
Every electronic circuit emits electromagnetic radiation correlated with its computations. Using a small antenna (near-field probe) placed close to a chip, attackers can capture these emissions and extract secret information — including encryption keys — without any physical contact with the circuit. EM attacks can sometimes work from centimetres away and can bypass shielding that protects against power analysis. Used against smart cards, embedded systems, and even processors.
Rowhammer Attack
💡 Analogy: Slamming a drawer repeatedly until the vibrations cause items in the drawer above to fall and rearrange themselves — without ever opening that drawer
A hardware vulnerability in DRAM (standard RAM). DRAM cells are so close together that repeatedly accessing ("hammering") one row of memory cells causes electromagnetic interference that flips bits (0→1 or 1→0) in adjacent rows — without ever accessing those rows. This corrupts data that was never touched. If the flipped bit is in a security-critical location (like a page table entry, a privilege flag, or a cryptographic key), the attacker gains unauthorised access or privileges. Demonstrated in 2015; remains relevant in 2024.
Foreshadow / SGAxe (SGX Attacks)
💡 Analogy: The bank vault we described in Chapter 8 turns out to have a ventilation shaft — it is still very secure, but not perfectly impenetrable
A series of attacks that broke Intel SGX enclaves using speculative execution (Foreshadow, 2018) or by exploiting the remote attestation process (SGAxe, 2020). These attacks showed that even hardware-level security mechanisms can be broken by adversarial research. The lesson for computer engineering: security must be continuously re-evaluated; no system should be assumed permanently secure. Intel issued microcode updates and redesigned subsequent processors to address these issues.
🎓 How to Teach This in 7 Minutes
  • Power Analysis: Electrician power meter analogy. "Your chip's power consumption reveals your password." (2 min)
  • EM Attack: "Hold an antenna near a chip — no contact needed — and steal the key." (2 min)
  • Rowhammer: Slamming drawer analogy. "Bit flip one security flag and suddenly you're the administrator." (3 min)

WK 10
Real-Time Systems — When Missing a Deadline is Catastrophic
⏱ ~7 min to teach
⌄
Real-TimeSystems
📌 What This Chapter Is About

Most software runs as fast as possible — but some software must complete tasks by precise deadlines, regardless of anything else. Aircraft flight control, anti-lock brakes, medical devices, and industrial robots are all real-time systems. This chapter covers the algorithms engineers use to schedule tasks with deadlines and guarantee they are always met.

📖 Your Teaching Summary (~7 min)

Imagine air traffic control software. It must update aircraft positions every 100ms — not sometimes, not usually, but always, without exception. Missing that deadline could cost lives. This is a Hard Real-Time System. Compare this to video streaming, where occasionally dropping a frame looks bad but nobody dies — that is a Soft Real-Time System.

The fundamental challenge: a processor can only do one thing at a time, but has many tasks with different deadlines. How do you decide which task runs when? This is the scheduling problem. The landmark solution came in a 1973 paper by Liu & Layland: Rate Monotonic Scheduling (RMS). The rule is beautifully simple: give higher priority to more frequently occurring tasks. If your brake system checks every 10ms and your instrument display updates every 100ms, the brake system always runs first. RMS can be mathematically proven to work as long as the processor is not overloaded beyond ~69% utilisation.

Priority Inversion is the famous bug that cost NASA the Mars Pathfinder mission in 1997: a low-priority task held a resource needed by a high-priority task, which then waited, causing the spacecraft's computer to keep resetting. Fixed with the Priority Inheritance Protocol.

🔑 Key Terms — Explained Simply
Hard Real-Time vs. Soft Real-Time
💡 Analogy: Hard = airbag that MUST deploy within 30ms or it is useless / Soft = Netflix buffering slightly — annoying but not fatal
Hard real-time systems: missing a deadline constitutes a system failure, regardless of how small the miss. Every single deadline must be met. Used in aircraft fly-by-wire, ABS brakes, pacemakers, industrial robots. Soft real-time systems: occasional deadline misses degrade quality but do not cause failure. Used in audio/video streaming, game physics, keyboard input. The distinction drives completely different design philosophies and hardware choices.
Rate Monotonic Scheduling (RMS)
💡 Analogy: At a hospital, the most frequently needed medication always gets priority in the pharmacy queue — simple rule, provably optimal
A fixed-priority scheduling algorithm: assign higher priority to tasks with shorter periods (higher frequency). Proved optimal for fixed-priority scheduling in 1973 by Liu and Layland. The schedulability test: sum all tasks' (execution_time / period) values — if this sum ≤ n × (2^(1/n) - 1), where n is the number of tasks, all deadlines are guaranteed. As n grows large, this approaches 69%. Simple, predictable, and widely used in RTOS implementations.
EDF — Earliest Deadline First
💡 Analogy: In a queue, whoever has the most urgent appointment next gets served first — priorities change dynamically throughout the day
A dynamic priority scheduling algorithm: at every moment, the task whose deadline is soonest gets the processor. Proved mathematically optimal — if any algorithm can schedule a set of tasks without missing deadlines, EDF can too. Can utilise up to 100% of CPU time (vs. ~69% for RMS). However, it is harder to implement in embedded systems because priorities change constantly and predicting worst-case behaviour is more complex.
Priority Inversion
💡 Analogy: The president is waiting for a key from a junior clerk, who is stuck behind a medium-level manager in a queue — the president waits, blocked by someone with lower authority
A situation where a high-priority task is blocked waiting for a resource held by a low-priority task, while medium-priority tasks run freely. This effectively inverts priorities. Caused the Mars Pathfinder spacecraft to repeatedly reset in 1997 — fixed mid-mission by NASA engineers uploading new scheduling parameters. Solution: Priority Inheritance Protocol — when a low-priority task holds a resource needed by a high-priority task, the low-priority task temporarily inherits the high priority until it releases the resource.
🎓 How to Teach This in 7 Minutes
  • Open with the airbag vs Netflix analogy — hard vs soft real-time. (1 min)
  • RMS: Hospital pharmacy analogy. "Most frequent task gets highest priority — mathematically proven correct." (3 min)
  • Priority Inversion: President and junior clerk analogy. "This bug crashed a Mars rover in 1997." (3 min)

WK 11
Quantum Computing — The Computer That Breaks Encryption
⏱ ~7 min to teach
⌄
Quantum
📌 What This Chapter Is About

Quantum computers are not just faster regular computers — they work on completely different physical principles. They can solve certain problems that classical computers would take millions of years to solve. This chapter explains how they work (without requiring physics knowledge), what they are good at, and why they are not replacing your laptop anytime soon.

📖 Your Teaching Summary (~7 min)

A regular computer bit is either 0 or 1 — like a light switch, either off or on. A quantum bit (qubit) is like a spinning coin: while it is spinning, it is both heads and tails simultaneously. Only when you stop it (measure it) does it "choose" one state. This is Superposition. A computer with 300 qubits in superposition can simultaneously represent 2^300 values — more than the number of atoms in the observable universe.

Entanglement means two qubits are linked: measuring one instantly determines the other's state, no matter how far apart they are. This allows quantum algorithms to process vast numbers of possibilities simultaneously and use clever interference to cancel wrong answers and amplify correct ones.

The practical impact: Shor's Algorithm can factor large numbers in polynomial time. RSA encryption (which secures your bank account) relies on factoring being effectively impossible classically. A large enough quantum computer would break RSA. We are not there yet — today's quantum computers are noisy (errors everywhere) and small. But this has already triggered a global race to develop Post-Quantum Cryptography — new encryption algorithms that quantum computers cannot break.

🔑 Key Terms — Explained Simply
Qubit (Quantum Bit)
💡 Analogy: A spinning coin — simultaneously heads AND tails until it lands. The landing is the measurement.
The basic unit of quantum information. Unlike a classical bit (0 or 1), a qubit exists in a superposition of both 0 and 1 simultaneously — described mathematically as α|0⟩ + β|1⟩ where |α|² and |β|² are the probabilities of measuring 0 or 1 respectively. Physical implementations include superconducting circuits cooled to near absolute zero (IBM, Google), trapped ions (IonQ), and photons. Each physical implementation has different advantages in coherence time, gate fidelity, and scalability.
Superposition
💡 Analogy: Searching a maze by sending a ghost that explores ALL paths simultaneously — the ghost collapses into one path only when it finds the exit
The quantum property that allows a qubit to be in multiple states simultaneously until measured. With n qubits in superposition, a quantum computer can represent 2^n states simultaneously. This is not the same as parallel computing (which uses multiple separate processors) — all 2^n states are encoded in the same physical system and processed together through quantum gates. The power comes from quantum interference: algorithms are designed to make wrong answers cancel each other out while correct answers reinforce.
Shor's Algorithm
💡 Analogy: Finding the combination to a safe by using quantum interference to eliminate all wrong answers simultaneously, leaving only the right one
A quantum algorithm invented in 1994 by Peter Shor that can factor large integers in polynomial time O((log N)³). On a classical computer, factoring a 2048-bit number would take longer than the age of the universe. RSA public-key encryption relies on this difficulty — if you can factor easily, you can break RSA. A fault-tolerant quantum computer with ~4,000 logical qubits could factor RSA-2048 in hours. Current machines have 1,000+ noisy physical qubits, needing ~1,000 physical qubits per logical qubit — so millions of physical qubits still needed for real threat.
Decoherence
💡 Analogy: Trying to balance a pencil perfectly on its tip — any vibration (from the environment) immediately topples it
The main engineering challenge in quantum computing. Qubits must maintain their fragile quantum state during computation, but any interaction with the environment (heat, vibration, electromagnetic noise, even a single stray photon) destroys the quantum state — the qubit "decoheres" and becomes a useless classical bit. Superconducting qubits must be cooled to 15 millikelvin (colder than outer space) to maintain coherence for microseconds to milliseconds. Quantum error correction codes can protect against decoherence but require many physical qubits per protected logical qubit.
NISQ — Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum
💡 Analogy: Early aeroplanes — they could fly short distances unreliably, not yet cross the Atlantic, but proving the concept was real
The current era of quantum computing, coined by physicist John Preskill in 2018. NISQ devices have 50–1,000 qubits but with significant noise (errors) and no error correction. They cannot run Shor's algorithm at useful scale. However, they can run "variational" algorithms (VQE for chemistry simulation, QAOA for optimisation) that work despite noise. The question researchers are investigating: is there any practical problem where NISQ devices outperform classical computers today?
🎓 How to Teach This in 7 Minutes
  • Spinning coin analogy — introduce qubit and superposition. "Not 0 or 1 — both at once until measured." (2 min)
  • Shor's Algorithm: "This algorithm would break every bank's encryption. We are 10–20 years away from that scale." (2 min)
  • Decoherence: Balancing pencil analogy. "This is why quantum computers run near absolute zero." (2 min)
  • NISQ: Early aeroplane analogy. "It works but we're still in the Wright Brothers phase." (1 min)

WK 12
Brain-Inspired Computing — Teaching Silicon to Think Like Neurons
⏱ ~7 min to teach
⌄
NeuromorphicAI/ML
📌 What This Chapter Is About

The human brain can do what no supercomputer can, at 20 watts of power. Neuromorphic computing tries to mimic how the brain works — not with continuous signals like regular AI, but with discrete electrical spikes like real neurons. This chapter explains what makes brain-inspired hardware different and why it matters for energy-efficient AI.

📖 Your Teaching Summary (~7 min)

Your brain has 86 billion neurons. At any moment, the vast majority are silent — only a tiny fraction fire at any given instant. This "sparsity" is why the brain uses only 20 watts while a data centre running a comparable AI model uses megawatts. The key difference: regular AI neurons send continuous signals (floating-point numbers) constantly. Biological neurons only fire occasional brief spikes — and only when they have something meaningful to communicate.

Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) mimic this: artificial neurons only fire discrete spikes, most of the time doing nothing (and consuming almost no power when silent). The challenge: since spikes are discrete (no gradient), you cannot use standard backpropagation to train them — this is an active research problem. Researchers use "surrogate gradients" (smooth approximations to spikes during training).

Intel's Loihi 2 chip implements 1 million neurons that can learn in real-time without a pre-training phase — like a biological brain learning on the fly. It is used for robotics, adaptive control, and pattern recognition at milliwatts of power. IBM's TrueNorth runs 1 million neurons at 65mW — for comparison, training GPT-4 consumed the equivalent power of thousands of homes for months.

🔑 Key Terms — Explained Simply
Neuromorphic Computing
💡 Analogy: Instead of building a faster calculator, building an artificial brain — circuits that work like neurons, not like arithmetic logic
A computing paradigm that directly mimics the structure and operation of biological neural systems. Neuromorphic chips use massively parallel, event-driven architectures where "neurons" communicate via discrete spikes only when needed. Because most neurons are silent most of the time, power consumption is proportional to activity — idle parts consume near-zero power. Pioneered by Carver Mead at Caltech in the late 1980s; now commercially available from Intel (Loihi), IBM (TrueNorth), and BrainScaleS (Heidelberg).
Spiking Neural Network (SNN)
💡 Analogy: Morse code vs. a phone call — Morse uses brief, sparse signals (dots and dashes) rather than continuous audio. Information is in the timing and pattern of spikes, not a continuous value.
An artificial neural network where neurons communicate through discrete spike events (binary pulse: fire or not fire) rather than continuous floating-point values. A neuron accumulates incoming spikes; when its "membrane potential" crosses a threshold, it fires a spike to its neighbours and resets. Information is encoded in the timing and rate of spikes. Enormously energy-efficient because computation only happens at spike events. Training SNNs is the main research challenge — standard backpropagation does not work on binary events.
STDP — Spike-Timing-Dependent Plasticity
💡 Analogy: If neuron A consistently fires just before neuron B causes it to fire, their connection strengthens — like learning by association ("neurons that fire together, wire together")
A biologically-inspired learning rule for SNNs: if a pre-synaptic neuron fires shortly before a post-synaptic neuron, the connection between them is strengthened (long-term potentiation). If the order is reversed, it is weakened. This "local" learning rule requires no global error signal — each synapse learns from only the timing of its neighbouring spikes. Intel's Loihi implements a programmable version of STDP for on-chip learning without needing to communicate with an external training system.
Memristors
💡 Analogy: A water pipe whose diameter permanently changes based on how much water has flowed through it — it "remembers" its history
A two-terminal electronic component whose resistance changes based on the history of current that has flowed through it — and it retains that resistance when power is off (non-volatile). This makes memristors natural artificial synapses: resistance encodes synaptic weight, and applying a voltage adjusts it. Memristive crossbar arrays can perform matrix-vector multiplication (core AI operation) directly in the storage medium, at extremely low energy. Companies like Knowm and IBM are developing memristive neuromorphic chips for ultra-low-power AI inference.
🎓 How to Teach This in 7 Minutes
  • "Your brain runs on 20 watts. A data centre running comparable AI uses megawatts. Why?" (1 min)
  • Sparsity: "Most neurons are silent. Only active neurons use power." (2 min)
  • SNNs: Morse code vs phone call analogy. "Spikes carry information through timing, not continuous values." (2 min)
  • Intel Loihi 2: "1 million neurons, learns in real time, milliwatts of power — used in robots." (2 min)

▸ PILLAR V & VI — AI SYSTEMS & AUTONOMOUS ENGINEERING (Weeks 13–16)
WK 13
Making AI Run Fast & Cheap — Systems Engineering for AI Models
⏱ ~7 min to teach
⌄
AI/MLArchitecture
📌 What This Chapter Is About

Building an AI model is only half the problem — making it run efficiently is the other half. This chapter covers the engineering techniques used to make large AI models (like GPT, Llama, Gemini) run faster, use less memory, and work on smaller devices. These are the techniques that make AI commercially viable.

📖 Your Teaching Summary (~7 min)

GPT-4 has over a trillion parameters (numbers). To run it, you need to load all those numbers from memory and multiply them in the right order. A single response might require trillions of arithmetic operations. Doing this efficiently requires engineering at every level — from how numbers are stored to how operations are scheduled on hardware.

Quantisation (from Chapter 6) is the first tool: instead of storing each AI weight as a 32-bit number (like writing 3.14159265), you round it to an 8-bit or even 4-bit number (like writing 3). The model becomes 4–8x smaller and runs proportionally faster, with accuracy dropping by only 1–2%. This is how models that required 8 GPUs now fit on one.

AI Compilers like TVM and XLA are software tools that take a model written in Python (TensorFlow, PyTorch) and translate it into highly optimised hardware instructions — fusing multiple operations together (so data never has to leave the fast cache), reordering operations for better memory access patterns, and generating code tailored to the specific chip being used. Think of them as translators between Python and machine code, but with aggressive optimisation steps in between.

LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) is a revolutionary fine-tuning technique: instead of updating a 70-billion parameter model (which costs millions of dollars), you insert small "adapter" matrices alongside the existing weights and train only those. You train 0.1% of the parameters, get 90% of the performance improvement, and the cost drops from millions to hundreds of dollars.

🔑 Key Terms — Explained Simply
KV-Cache (Key-Value Cache)
💡 Analogy: When writing a long document, you keep your previous sentences in front of you so you do not re-read the entire document every time you write a new sentence
In transformer-based AI models (like GPT), every new word generated needs to "attend" to all previous words. Without caching, this means re-computing the relationships between all words from scratch for every new word — extremely wasteful. The KV-cache stores the intermediate computation results (Key and Value matrices) for all previous tokens, so only the new token needs fresh computation. The problem: the KV-cache grows linearly with context length. A 128K-token context window might need gigabytes of cache — a major memory management challenge for deployment.
AI Compiler (TVM, XLA, MLIR)
💡 Analogy: An expert translator who not only translates your speech, but also reorganises your sentences to be maximally efficient in the new language
Software frameworks that take an AI model (defined in Python using PyTorch/TensorFlow) and compile it into optimised machine code for specific hardware (GPU, TPU, CPU, FPGA). Key optimisations: operator fusion (combining multiple operations like MatMul + GELU into one kernel, avoiding intermediate memory writes), memory planning (minimising peak memory usage), and hardware-aware tiling (splitting computations into blocks sized for cache efficiency). Without a good compiler, the same model can run 10x slower on the same hardware.
LoRA — Low-Rank Adaptation
💡 Analogy: Instead of re-painting an entire building, you stick post-it notes on specific walls that modify the appearance — much cheaper and reversible
A fine-tuning technique for large AI models. Rather than updating all model weights (billions of parameters, hugely expensive), LoRA inserts small pairs of matrices (B and A, where B×A approximates the change needed) alongside the frozen original weights. The product B×A has a much smaller number of parameters than the original weight matrix — this is the "low-rank" part. The model trains only B and A (often < 0.1% of original parameters) while the original weights are frozen. After fine-tuning, B×A can be merged back into the original weights. Used to fine-tune LLMs on consumer GPUs in hours instead of on supercomputers over weeks.
Operator Fusion
💡 Analogy: Instead of cooking each ingredient separately and putting them on different plates before combining, you cook them together in one pot — fewer dishes, faster, less waste
A compiler optimisation that combines multiple adjacent AI operations (like matrix multiplication followed by a normalisation step followed by an activation function) into a single GPU kernel, rather than running them as separate steps. Each step normally writes its output to GPU memory and reads it back for the next step — this memory traffic is the bottleneck. By fusing them, intermediate results stay in the GPU's fast registers or shared memory, never touching slow HBM. Flash Attention is the famous example: fusing the entire multi-head attention mechanism reduces memory usage from O(N²) to O(N).
🎓 How to Teach This in 7 Minutes
  • "GPT-4 has a trillion parameters. Getting it to respond in seconds is serious engineering." (1 min)
  • Quantisation: Rounding 3.14159 to 3 — model becomes 4x smaller, barely less accurate. (2 min)
  • LoRA: Post-it note analogy — fine-tune 0.1% of parameters for 90% of the benefit. (2 min)
  • AI Compilers: Expert translator analogy — converts Python models to hardware-optimised code. (2 min)

WK 14
Self-Driving Cars & Robots — When Computers Control the Physical World
⏱ ~7 min to teach
⌄
SystemsReal-Time
📌 What This Chapter Is About

Cyber-Physical Systems (CPS) are systems where computation is tightly connected to the physical world — self-driving cars, industrial robots, surgical systems, smart grids. Errors are not just software bugs — they cause physical harm. This chapter covers the middleware, networking, and verification tools used to make these systems safe and reliable.

📖 Your Teaching Summary (~7 min)

A regular software bug causes an app to crash. A bug in a self-driving car causes a crash of a different kind. Cyber-Physical Systems are different from regular software because physical processes are continuous and uncertain, but computers are discrete and deterministic. Bridging this gap safely is the core challenge.

ROS 2 (Robot Operating System 2) is the standard software framework for robotics. It provides a communication backbone where different components of a robot (camera processing, motor control, path planning) publish and subscribe to data topics — similar to MQTT from Chapter 7, but with real-time guarantees. Used by NASA rovers, surgical robots, autonomous vehicles, and warehouse robots.

Time-Sensitive Networking (TSN) extends regular Ethernet (which was designed for "best effort" delivery — fast but unpredictable timing) with deterministic timing guarantees. In a car, brake-by-wire signals cannot be delayed by video streaming traffic — TSN uses time-aware traffic schedulers to guarantee that critical messages always arrive within microseconds, sharing the same cable as regular data traffic.

A Digital Twin is a live simulation that mirrors a real physical system in real time using sensor data. Engineers can test "what if the temperature sensor fails?" on the digital twin without risking the real system.

🔑 Key Terms — Explained Simply
Cyber-Physical System (CPS)
💡 Analogy: A building's air conditioning that not only measures temperature but directly controls machinery based on those measurements — computation and physics are inseparable
A system where computer algorithms are tightly coupled with physical processes — not just monitoring but actively controlling. Examples: autonomous vehicles (perception → decision → actuation loop), smart grids (real-time power balancing across millions of nodes), surgical robots (sub-millimetre precision requiring real-time force feedback), and industrial process control. CPS design must account for physical dynamics (inertia, delays, noise), not just logic. Correctness depends on timing — the right answer at the wrong time is wrong.
ROS 2 — Robot Operating System 2
💡 Analogy: A telephone exchange for robot components — any part of the robot can talk to any other part without needing to know who else is listening
An open-source middleware framework (not actually an operating system) for building robotic systems. Components (nodes) communicate by publishing messages to named "topics" — a camera node publishes images, a detection node subscribes and publishes detected objects, a planner subscribes and publishes trajectories. Built on DDS (Data Distribution Service), which provides real-time, reliable, configurable Quality-of-Service communication. Used in industrial robots, autonomous vehicles (Apollo by Baidu), space applications (NASA JPL), and research robots worldwide.
TSN — Time-Sensitive Networking (IEEE 802.1)
💡 Analogy: A managed intersection where ambulances always get a green light even during heavy traffic — critical messages are never delayed by regular traffic
A set of IEEE 802.1 standards that add determinism to standard Ethernet. Regular Ethernet is "best effort" — it sends packets as fast as possible but gives no timing guarantees. TSN adds mechanisms: time synchronisation across all network devices (IEEE 802.1AS), traffic shaping (IEEE 802.1Qbv — time-aware shaper that gates different traffic classes at specific time slots), and frame preemption (IEEE 802.1Qbu — lets critical frames interrupt lower-priority frames mid-transmission). Used in automotive (AUTOSAR), industrial automation, professional audio/video, and 5G fronthaul networks.
Digital Twin
💡 Analogy: A live, perfectly-synchronised virtual replica of a real aircraft engine — engineers can simulate failures and optimisations without touching the real engine
A real-time virtual model of a physical system, continuously updated with sensor data from the real system, that mirrors its current state and can predict future states. Initially developed by NASA for spacecraft monitoring; now used in manufacturing (Siemens), smart cities, wind farms, and hospitals. Enables predictive maintenance (detect failures before they happen), performance optimisation (test changes virtually before physical implementation), and operator training (simulate emergency scenarios). The twin is not a recording — it is a live simulation that diverges from the real system only when reality does something unexpected.
🎓 How to Teach This in 7 Minutes
  • "Software bugs crash apps. CPS bugs crash cars. Physical consequences change everything." (1 min)
  • ROS 2: Telephone exchange analogy — camera publishes, brake control subscribes, all coordinated. (2 min)
  • TSN: Managed intersection analogy — "Brake signals always get the green light even when video is streaming on the same cable." (2 min)
  • Digital Twin: Virtual aircraft engine analogy — "Test failure scenarios without risk." (2 min)

WK 15
5G Networks — Not Just Faster Mobile, But a New Computing Platform
⏱ ~7 min to teach
⌄
SystemsEdge/IoT
📌 What This Chapter Is About

5G is much more than "faster 4G." It is a fundamentally new network architecture designed to serve radically different use cases simultaneously — from streaming 8K video to controlling remote surgery to connecting a billion IoT sensors. This chapter explains the key 5G innovations and how they connect back to edge computing and computer engineering.

📖 Your Teaching Summary (~7 min)

Think of 4G as a single-lane toll road — it works, but everyone shares the same lane and the same rules. 5G is like redesigning the entire transport infrastructure: motorways for high-speed traffic, dedicated lanes for emergency vehicles, and special pathways for slow-moving heavy goods. The three pillars of 5G serve three completely different needs:

(1) eMBB (Enhanced Mobile Broadband) — very high throughput for your phone and home broadband, using mmWave radio (very high frequency, very high speed but short range — requires many small antennas). (2) URLLC (Ultra-Reliable Low-Latency Communications) — < 1ms latency and 99.9999% reliability for autonomous vehicles, remote surgery, and factory robots. (3) mMTC (Massive Machine-Type Communications) — millions of IoT sensors per square kilometre, each sending tiny amounts of data very infrequently.

Network Slicing is the magic that makes this possible: using software, operators can carve the physical 5G infrastructure into multiple isolated virtual networks, each with different performance guarantees — one slice for hospitals (extreme reliability), one for broadband (high throughput), one for smart meters (low power, low data). From a computer engineering perspective, this is virtualisation applied to network infrastructure — the same concept as virtual machines, but for entire networks.

🔑 Key Terms — Explained Simply
5G Three Service Categories
💡 Analogy: An airport with three completely different departure terminals — passenger flights, cargo planes, and private jets — each optimised for different needs on the same runway system
eMBB (Enhanced Mobile Broadband): High speed, high data rate — streaming, VR, fixed wireless broadband. Targets > 1 Gbps peak. Uses massive MIMO and millimetre-wave spectrum. URLLC (Ultra-Reliable Low-Latency Communications): Reliability 99.9999%, latency < 1ms. For autonomous vehicles, remote surgery, industrial automation. The latency requirement is so tight that edge computing (from Chapter 6) is necessary — the signal cannot travel to a distant data centre. mMTC (Massive Machine-Type Communications): Up to 1 million devices per km². Low power, low data rate, long battery life. For smart cities, agriculture, utilities.
Network Slicing
💡 Analogy: One physical motorway system, but with different virtual "lanes" booked for different vehicles — ambulances always have a guaranteed lane, even during rush hour
A 5G feature that uses Software-Defined Networking (SDN) and Network Function Virtualisation (NFV) to create multiple isolated virtual networks on a shared physical infrastructure. Each slice has its own guaranteed bandwidth, latency, and reliability characteristics. A hospital operator can reserve a slice with 99.9999% reliability for medical devices; a gaming company can reserve a low-latency slice; a utility company can reserve a slice for millions of smart meters. Operators can create, resize, and destroy slices in software without touching hardware.
Massive MIMO
💡 Analogy: Instead of a loudspeaker pointing everywhere, imagine 256 individually-steerable directional speakers — each aimed precisely at a different listener simultaneously
Multiple-Input Multiple-Output (MIMO) with 64–256 antenna elements at the base station (compared to 2–8 in 4G). Using beamforming — precise mathematical steering of radio signals by coordinating phase differences across all antennas — the base station can direct focused beams at individual users, dramatically increasing signal strength and spatial reuse. Multiple users can use the same frequency simultaneously as long as they are in different spatial directions. This is the primary reason 5G can serve so many users with high throughput in the same area.
O-RAN — Open Radio Access Network
💡 Analogy: Breaking apart a proprietary telephone exchange so anyone can build compatible equipment — like open-sourcing the entire mobile network infrastructure
A movement to disaggregate and open the interfaces of mobile network base stations, traditionally supplied as closed, proprietary systems from single vendors (Ericsson, Nokia, Huawei). O-RAN separates the Radio Unit (antenna hardware), Distributed Unit (real-time signal processing), and Central Unit (non-real-time functions) with open interfaces between them. This allows operators to mix vendors and enables software-defined intelligent controllers ("xApps") that use AI/ML to dynamically optimise network performance. Backed by a consortium of 300+ operators and vendors.

📌 What This Chapter Is About

The final chapter synthesises all six pillars of the course into a coherent narrative about where computer engineering is heading. 📖 Your Teaching Summary (~7 min)

Step back and see the pattern across all 16 weeks. The single unifying theme: the era of free performance from transistor scaling is over. Every topic in this course is a different response to that ending.

Because we cannot make single cores faster (Ch.1–3), we build heterogeneous systems with specialised chips (Ch.4–5). Because cloud computing is too slow or expensive for everything, we push intelligence to the edge (Ch.6–7). Because our hardware optimisations created security vulnerabilities, we build trusted hardware (Ch.8–9). Because software timing matters as much as logic in physical systems, we engineer for real-time guarantees (Ch.10). Because classical computing will eventually hit quantum limitations, we research post-classical alternatives (Ch.11–12). Because AI models are the dominant workload, we optimise every layer for AI efficiency (Ch.13). Because computing is now inseparable from the physical world, we design systems that bridge computation and physics safely (Ch.14–15).

The engineering lesson: you cannot optimise one layer in isolation. A hardware security fix costs performance. A real-time constraint limits which AI models you can use. A network slicing policy affects edge computing feasibility. The best computer engineers think across all layers simultaneously — from silicon to application, from hardware to network.

🔑 Big Picture Synthesis — The 6 Pillars and Their Connections
Why Architecture Matters to Security (Ch.2–3 → Ch.8–9)
💡 The performance feature that made CPUs fast became the attack vector that made them vulnerable
Speculative execution (Chapter 3) was a performance breakthrough. It became the root cause of Spectre and Meltdown (Chapter 8). The Roofline Model (Chapter 3) explains why memory bandwidth is the bottleneck — and memory bandwidth is exactly what side-channel attackers exploit (Chapter 9). Architecture decisions made for performance have deep, unforeseen security consequences. This is why security must be designed in at the silicon level, not bolted on in software.
Why Edge Computing Needs Real-Time Systems (Ch.6 → Ch.10 → Ch.14)
💡 Moving computation to the edge is only useful if the edge can meet the timing guarantees that physical systems require
Edge computing (Chapter 6) is motivated by latency requirements — you cannot send data to the cloud and back fast enough. But at the edge, systems must be real-time (Chapter 10) — missing a deadline in a robot or vehicle has physical consequences. ROS 2 and TSN (Chapter 14) provide the middleware and network infrastructure to meet those deadlines. TinyML (Chapter 6) must fit within the power and latency constraints set by real-time requirements. These topics are not separate — they form a continuous design chain.
Why Quantum Matters to Security (Ch.11 → Ch.8)
💡 A future technology (quantum computing) is reshaping how we design security systems today
Shor's Algorithm (Chapter 11) threatens RSA and ECC encryption (Chapter 8). Even though large-scale quantum computers do not exist yet, organisations are already migrating to post-quantum cryptography — new encryption algorithms designed to be secure against quantum attacks. The TPM 2.0 (Chapter 8) and secure enclaves need to be updated to use quantum-resistant algorithms. The threat is not immediate but the migration timeline is 10–20 years — and data encrypted today could be harvested now and decrypted later (harvest-now-decrypt-later attacks). This is why NIST standardised post-quantum cryptography algorithms in 2024.
The Next Decade of Computer Engineering
💡 From a single-core world to a specialised, heterogeneous, edge-first, security-by-design, AI-everywhere world
The defining trends for the next 10 years: (1) Chiplets become the standard — heterogeneous integration replaces monolithic chips. (2) AI is embedded everywhere — from microcontrollers (TinyML) to data centre accelerators (TPUs, H100). (3) Quantum error correction progresses — practical advantage for specific problems (drug discovery, materials science, cryptography) becomes real. (4) Security is silicon-level — hardware roots of trust and TEEs become standard in all processors. (5) 5G + edge + AI converge — the base station becomes an edge AI node. (6) CPS safety standards mature — formal verification and digital twins become standard practice for safety-critical systems.
